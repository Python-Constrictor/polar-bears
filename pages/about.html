A Polar Bear's Habitat


Contrary to popular belief, the Polar Bears live in the Arctic region throughout the U.S., Canada, Greenland, and Norway. They spend most of their time on sea ice where they camp holes to hunt for seals, create dens for their young and travel to mate.

A Polar Bear's Physical Adaptations


Polar bears have hollow, translucent fur. This allows for it to reflect all visible light, which gives it a white appearance that aids in camouflage. Moreover, the hollow fur also helps them trap heat. They also have a thick layer of fat, called blubber, which acts as additional insulation and food reserves. Additionally, the paws of a polar bear are large and webbed, acting as "snowshoes" and "paddles." This allows for the polar bear to travel on ice and in water.

A Polar Bear's Behavioral Traits


Contrary to most bears and mammals, polar bears do not truly hibernate. As the ice cover is greater during winter months, it is easier for polar bears to hunt so they can store fat reserves for the summer. They also hunt alone because their prey, the ringed seal, is often solitary. Therefore they are less likely to notice approaching predators, making an ambush tactic more efficient.

A Polar Bear's Diet


Polar bears primarily eat seals for their fat content. They hunt by breaking into seal dens, ambushing seals that surfaced to rest on ice, and camping at seal breathing holes.
